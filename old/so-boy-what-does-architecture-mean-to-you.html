I wrote this in May, but I withdrew it following the advice of a very good friend that it wasn't very well written, which almost certainly translates to "not very well thought out"! Maybe this version is a little better."

This will almost certainly upset a lot of people. It ties in with the thinking that leads me to some of my (slightly rushed) comments here about architects' pay, and about my general feeling about our collective view of our profession and our professional identity.


I've often heard people telling tales of being asked by a seasoned practitioner "so what does architecture mean to you", on their first day in an office. The usual answer is something like Grasshopper gives at the beginning of kung fu, either something naive or something lifted from a book without too much thought because it sounds good.

I've been thinking about this question for a long time because none of the answers I've ever heard were particularly satisfactory. Nothing was simple enough to be comprehensible, nor complete enough to be satisfactory; which has lead me to having a crack at defining what it means to me.


If you were to ask people what was in this photo, just general people who were walking past, you'd probably get a number of different responses, but I bet you that the top five would have: Sydney, buildings, architecture and skyline amongst it somewhere.


If we were to try the same test on the same bunch of general punters then you might get blood, bear, children, medicine, but you almost certainly wouldn't get industrial design or product design. This thought experiment leads me to one of the emerging themes in my moaning about the world; failure of definitions. (I've got a few more posts in the pipeline about that, but it relies on my motivation...)

Architecture is both a noun and a verb, and whilst I'm happy with the general public using it both ways, I feel that as a body of professionals we ought to be specific in how we apply it. This is my attempt to be specific.


In my formulation of a rough meaning, the skyline shot above is composed of buildings. There are just too many different disciplines involved in the creation of any building much bigger than a garden shed to call it architecture. Doing so indicates a horrific level of arrogance and disrespect for non-architect contributors to the project.

It would seem odd to refer to the skyline as a photo of structural engineering, town planning, politics, concrete mixing or contract writing. Yet they are all constituent parts of the huge team that was responsible for that skyline coming into existence. So why should architecture be privileged above all others? This is a question that I don't feel I'm in a position to answer, there is too much history, and I'm still too young to make any sort of attempt that won't just come across as naïve.

I think that architecture is a process; a verb. I don't think that too many people would disagree that when someone who self-identifies as 'an architect' goes to work, they do things that they would identify as 'architecture'. I see architecture (verb) to be an organisational role; the task of martialling and synthesising large quantities of information to resolve (often conflicting) constraints. The resolution of those constraints is the product of the process of architecture.

The debate about protection of function and the less onerous, but just as hotly contested one on protection of title has often poked an angry stick at 'software architects' as being some sort of joke profession, stealing the title architect as a way to make them sound sexier or more respectable. What they do is conceptually very closely aligned with the role that I think that architects do. They also need resolve complex networks of interdependent constraints and, like (building) architects, their product is a design that is then brought to the end user by a proxy; the software engineer (or in the built environment, the builder).

I think that this is a fairly simple concept, and I hope it won't result in my excommunication from too many circles. So to summarise:

  • Architecture is a verb, not a noun.
  • There is no such thing as a piece of architecture, simply a product that is the outcome of a process that architecture was performed during its inception.
  • As architecture is a process, its bounds are not limited to the built environment. Any area that the application of these skills would be beneficial could just as validly use the process.

This seems pretty simple to me, but it has some implications for the protection of name argument that I don't really want to get into, but to put them to bed before they get out of control, I think that there needs to be more made of affiliation to a professional body rather than reliance on an international word to describe why someone ought to hire you or someone of your tribe, and not anyone from different tribe, or heaven forbid a member of no tribe at all.
